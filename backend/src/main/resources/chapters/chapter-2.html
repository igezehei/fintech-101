Fundamental analysis is the process of determining the intrinsic value of an asset by analyzing the underlying business, industry, and macroeconomic conditions. A fundamentally-oriented investor asks: Is this asset worth more or less than what the market is currently pricing it at?

Learning Objectives

  • Read and interpret the three core financial statements
  • Apply valuation metrics to assess whether a stock is cheap or expensive
  • Understand how macroeconomic conditions shape asset selection
  • Build a logical framework for selecting individual stocks
  • Design a diversified portfolio based on your goals and risk tolerance
  • Explain compounding and quantify its power

2.1 What Is Fundamental Analysis?

Fundamental analysis is the primary approach of long-term investors like Warren Buffett. It is less useful for short-term trading (hours/days), where technical analysis and market structure dominate.

  • Top-down: Start with the macroeconomy, drill into sectors, then select individual companies.
  • Bottom-up: Start with an individual company and assess whether it stands on its own merit regardless of macro.

2.2 Reading Financial Statements

The Income Statement (Profit & Loss)

Tells you how much money a company made or lost over a period of time.

  • Revenue (Top line): Total sales — how much came in the door.
  • Gross Profit: Revenue minus Cost of Goods Sold.
  • Net Income (Bottom line): What's left after all expenses, taxes, and interest.
  • EPS: Net income divided by shares outstanding. Investors focus heavily on EPS growth.

NVIDIA Example: In fiscal year 2024, NVIDIA reported ~$60B revenue and ~$29B net income — a net margin above 48%. Compare to Intel in the same period: similar revenue, much thinner margins, and massive capex with uncertain returns.

The Balance Sheet

A snapshot of the company's financial position at a single point in time: Assets − Liabilities = Shareholders' Equity.

  • Debt-to-Equity (D/E): High D/E means more financial risk.
  • Current Ratio: Current assets / current liabilities. Should generally be above 1.0.

The Cash Flow Statement

Free Cash Flow (FCF) = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditures. FCF is arguably the most important metric for long-term investors. A profitable business on paper can still have negative OCF if it's burning through working capital.

2.3 Valuation Metrics

  • P/E Ratio: Stock Price / EPS. The S&P 500 historically trades at 15–20x in normal conditions.
  • PEG Ratio: P/E / EPS Growth Rate. Below 1.0 is often considered undervalued relative to growth.
  • EV/EBITDA: Accounts for the full cost of acquiring a company. Widely used in M&A.
  • DCF: A business is worth the present value of all future cash flows. When interest rates rise, the discount rate rises — this is why high-growth stocks get hit hard when rates rise.

2.4 Macroeconomic Conditions and Asset Selection

The Economic Cycle

  • Expansion: GDP rising, earnings growing. Risk assets (stocks, real estate) perform well.
  • Peak: Inflation rises. Late-cycle sectors (energy, materials) outperform.
  • Contraction/Recession: Defensive sectors (healthcare, utilities, staples) and bonds outperform.
  • Trough: Central banks cut rates. Early-cycle sectors (tech, financials) recover first.

Key rule: Low/falling rates favor growth stocks and long-duration bonds. High/rising rates favor value stocks, financials, commodities, and cash.

The yield curve: An inverted 2-year vs. 10-year yield has preceded nearly every US recession in the last 50 years. The lag is typically 12–18 months.

2.5 Stock Selection Criteria

Quantitative Factors

  • Revenue growth — is the top line growing consistently?
  • Earnings growth — is EPS growing, ideally faster than revenue (margin expansion)?
  • Free cash flow generation — is the business generating real cash?
  • Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) — ROIC above cost of capital means value is being created.

Qualitative Factors

  • Business model clarity — do you understand exactly how this company makes money?
  • Competitive position — what prevents competitors from taking this company's customers?
  • Management quality — are they capital-allocators who have created value over time?
  • Market size (TAM) — is this a large, growing opportunity?

2.6 Investment Strategies

  • Value Investing: Buy assets trading below intrinsic value. Key metrics: low P/E, low P/B. Practitioners: Benjamin Graham, Warren Buffett.
  • Growth Investing: Buy companies with above-average growth potential, often at a premium. Most sensitive to interest rate increases.
  • Dividend Investing: Focus on companies paying consistent, growing dividends. Key metrics: yield, payout ratio, FCF coverage.
  • Index Investing: VOO is the purest example — buy the entire S&P 500 at 0.03% cost. Research shows most actively managed funds underperform their index over 10+ years.

2.7 Portfolio Design and Diversification

Diversification spreads investments so that poor performance of any single asset has limited impact. Adding stocks reduces idiosyncratic risk up to about 20–30 holdings; beyond that, additional diversification has diminishing returns.

  • 60/40 portfolio: 60% equities, 40% bonds — the traditional balanced portfolio.
  • Sectors: The S&P 500 has 11 sectors. Holding across sectors reduces sector-specific risk.
  • Rebalancing: Periodically sell what has grown and buy what has lagged to restore target allocation.

2.8 The Power of Compounding

Formula: Future Value = Present Value × (1 + rate)^years

Example: $10,000 invested in VOO at 10% average annual return:

  • After 10 years: $25,937
  • After 20 years: $67,275
  • After 30 years: $174,494
  • After 40 years: $452,593

A Wealthsimple account with $500/month contributions into a VOO equivalent at 9% return over 30 years yields approximately $900,000 from $180,000 in contributions. That's $720,000 of compound growth.

Time in the market vs. timing the market: Missing just the best 10 trading days per decade dramatically reduces returns. The best days often follow the worst — exactly when fear is highest.

2.9 Economic Moats and Zombie Companies

Types of Moats

  • Network effects: Product becomes more valuable as more people use it (Visa, Mastercard, NVIDIA's CUDA ecosystem).
  • Cost advantages: Ability to produce at lower cost than competitors (Amazon logistics, Walmart scale).
  • Switching costs: The cost of switching to a competitor is high (Salesforce, SAP).
  • Intangible assets: Patents, licenses, brands.

Zombie Companies

A zombie company earns just enough to service its debt but cannot grow or invest meaningfully. They look cheap on paper but have no path to value creation — a classic value trap. The era of near-zero interest rates (2009–2021) created many zombies. When rates rose in 2022, many faced existential stress.
