By the end of this chapter, you will be able to:

  • Explain what a financial system is and why it exists

1.1 The Financial System: What It Is and Why It Exists

At its most basic, a financial system is a mechanism for moving money from those who have it to those who need it, at a price both sides can agree on.

Imagine a farmer who wants to build a barn but doesn't have the capital. Somewhere else, a retired teacher has savings sitting idle in a bank account earning almost nothing. The financial system connects them. The teacher's money — channeled through a bank, a bond, or a fund — becomes the farmer's loan. The teacher earns interest. The farmer gets the barn. Both are better off.

Now scale that idea up to millions of participants, hundreds of countries, trillions of dollars, and millions of instruments — and you have the modern global financial system.

What the financial system actually does:

  1. Capital allocation — moves money from savers to productive uses
  2. Price discovery — through competition, markets find what something is actually worth
  3. Risk transfer — allows participants to share, shift, or hedge risk
  4. Liquidity provision — ensures that buyers and sellers can transact when they want to
  5. Information aggregation — prices incorporate the collective knowledge and expectations of all participants

Markets don't move randomly (though they sometimes feel that way). Prices move because of changing expectations. When new information arrives — an earnings report, an interest rate decision, a geopolitical event — market participants update their expectations about the future, and prices adjust. Understanding this helps you read price behavior rather than just reacting to it.

1.2 The Main Players in Financial Markets

To understand a market, you need to understand who is in it and what they want.

Retail Investors

These are individual people — you, your neighbor, someone in their living room with a brokerage app. Retail investors tend to have smaller account sizes, longer time horizons (at least ideally), less information and speed than institutions, and more behavioral bias. A retail investor in Canada might open a Wealthsimple account and buy 10 shares of VOO. They represent a large proportion of market participants by count, but a relatively small percentage of total volume.

Institutional Investors

These are organizations that manage large pools of capital on behalf of clients or beneficiaries. They include:

  • Mutual funds — pool retail investor money and invest it per a stated mandate
  • Pension funds — manage retirement savings (e.g., CPPIB in Canada, CalPERS in the US)
  • Hedge funds — less regulated, higher risk, often use leverage and derivatives
  • Sovereign wealth funds — government-controlled funds (e.g., Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, worth over $1.5 trillion)
  • Insurance companies — invest premiums until they're needed for claims
  • Endowments — manage the long-term assets of universities and nonprofits (e.g., Yale, Harvard)

Institutions move massive amounts of money. A hedge fund moving into a position can take days or weeks to build without moving the market against themselves. This is why they leave footprints in the price — and why understanding their behavior is central to advanced trading.

Market Makers

Market makers are firms or individuals who continuously provide buy and sell quotes for an instrument. They profit from the bid-ask spread — the difference between what they'll buy at and what they'll sell at. If a stock is quoted at $99.90 bid / $100.00 ask, the market maker earns $0.10 per share if they buy from one trader and sell to another in the same moment. Market makers also take on inventory risk — their hedging activity can move prices in predictable ways (more on this in the options chapter). Major market makers include Citadel Securities, Virtu Financial, and Jane Street.

Central Banks

Central banks are the most powerful actors in global financial markets. They control:

  • Interest rates — raising rates makes borrowing more expensive, slowing the economy; cutting rates stimulates it
  • Money supply — through quantitative easing (QE) and quantitative tightening (QT)
  • Currency value — through foreign exchange interventions

The US Federal Reserve (the Fed), European Central Bank (ECB), Bank of Japan (BOJ), and Bank of Canada (BOC) are the major ones. If there is one macro habit worth building: watch central bank policy. It's the single most important macro driver of asset prices.

Commercial Banks and Investment Banks

  • Commercial banks (e.g., TD, JPMorgan Chase) take deposits, make loans, and facilitate payments.
  • Investment banks (e.g., Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley) help companies raise capital through IPOs and bond offerings, advise on M&A, and run large trading desks.

Corporations

Companies issue stocks and bonds to raise capital. Their financial performance — revenue, earnings, guidance — drives stock prices. When NVIDIA reports a blowout earnings quarter, its stock moves because investors update their expectations about the company's future cash flows.

Brokerages and Exchanges

  • Brokerages — the interface between investors and markets. Examples: Wealthsimple, Fidelity, Interactive Brokers, Schwab.
  • Exchanges — where trading happens: NYSE, NASDAQ, TSX (Toronto), LSE (London), CME (futures). They set rules, provide infrastructure, and ensure trades are settled.

1.3 Financial Instruments: What You Can Actually Buy

A financial instrument is a tradeable claim on something of value. There are five main categories:

1. Equities (Stocks)

Owning a share of stock means owning a fractional ownership stake in a company. Shareholders benefit from capital appreciation, dividends, and voting rights. If you own 100 shares of NVIDIA (NVDA) at $900, you own a tiny fraction of the business. If NVIDIA doubles its earnings, the market is likely to reprice the stock higher.

2. Fixed Income (Bonds)

A bond is a loan from the investor to the issuer. The issuer pays periodic interest (the coupon) and returns the principal at maturity.

  • Government bonds — US Treasuries, Canadian bonds (considered very low risk)
  • Corporate bonds — issued by companies; higher yield, higher risk
  • Municipal bonds — issued by local governments; often tax-advantaged

Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions. The 10-year Treasury yield is the benchmark against which all other assets are priced.

3. Currencies (Forex)

The forex market trades currency pairs — EUR/USD, USD/JPY, USD/CAD. It is the largest financial market in the world: over $7.5 trillion traded per day, operating 24 hours a day, 5 days a week. Currencies are influenced by interest rate differentials, inflation, political stability, and current account balances.

4. Commodities

Physical goods that are traded: gold, silver, oil (WTI and Brent), natural gas, agricultural products, copper. Gold is widely used as an inflation hedge and safe haven. Oil is a barometer of global economic activity. Copper is sometimes called "Dr. Copper" because its price often reflects economic health.

5. Derivatives

Derivatives are contracts whose value is derived from an underlying asset:

  • Options — the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an asset at a specified price
  • Futures — the obligation to buy or sell an asset at a future date and price
  • Swaps — agreements to exchange cash flows (e.g., interest rate swaps)

Derivatives serve two main purposes: hedging (reducing risk) and speculation (amplifying potential gains — and losses). Covered extensively in Chapter 13.

Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)

An ETF is a basket of securities that trades on an exchange like a single stock. VOO is the Vanguard S&P 500 ETF — one share gives you proportional exposure to all 500 companies in the S&P 500 at a 0.03% expense ratio. ETFs have democratized investing, giving any retail investor access to entire markets for a very low cost.

1.4 Global Market Size: How Big Is This World?

Understanding scale helps you understand relative importance and how different markets interact.

Market Approximate Size (2024–2025)
Global equity markets~$110 trillion
Global bond markets~$130 trillion
Foreign exchange (daily turnover)~$7.5 trillion / day
Global derivatives (notional value)~$700+ trillion
Gold market~$14 trillion (total above-ground gold)
Crypto markets~$2–3 trillion (highly variable)
Real estate (global)~$350+ trillion

The US equity market (NYSE + NASDAQ) represents roughly 45–50% of global equity market capitalization. The S&P 500 accounts for roughly 80% of US equity market cap.

Why does market size matter?

  • Liquidity: Larger markets are harder to manipulate and easier to trade in (tighter spreads, faster execution).
  • Impact: Signals from large markets (US stocks, US Treasuries) reverberate globally.
  • Opportunity: Size reflects where capital flows — and where capital flows creates price action.

1.5 How Markets Are Structured and Where Trading Happens

Primary vs. Secondary Markets

  • Primary market: Where new securities are issued. A company going public through an IPO sells shares in the primary market and receives the proceeds.
  • Secondary market: Where existing securities trade between investors. When you buy NVIDIA on the NYSE, you're buying from another investor — NVIDIA doesn't receive that money.

Exchanges vs. Over-the-Counter (OTC) Markets

  • Exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ, CME) are centralized platforms with standardized rules and publicly visible prices.
  • OTC markets are decentralized networks of dealers trading directly with each other. Most bond, currency, and derivatives trading happens OTC.

Key Market Sessions

  • Asia (Tokyo): ~7 PM – 4 AM ET
  • London: ~3 AM – 12 PM ET
  • New York: ~8 AM – 5 PM ET
  • London–NY overlap: 8 AM – 12 PM ET — highest volume, most opportunity

Common Mistakes

1. Thinking the stock market is the economy. The market prices in future expectations, not current reality. In 2020, markets rallied sharply even while economic data was still terrible — because markets priced in the eventual recovery before it happened.

2. Underestimating institutional influence. Institutions have more information, faster execution, better tools, and fewer behavioral biases. Understanding who you're trading against matters.

3. Ignoring macro. Individual stock pickers who ignore central bank policy, interest rates, and economic cycles often get run over by forces they didn't see coming.

4. Confusing trading with investing. These are different activities with different timeframes, risk profiles, and tools. Confusion leads to doing neither well.

Chapter Summary

Financial markets exist to move capital from savers to users efficiently. The main participants are retail investors, institutional investors, market makers, central banks, corporations, and brokerages. The main asset classes are equities, bonds, currencies, commodities, and derivatives — each with different risk and return characteristics. The global financial system is enormous — equities alone represent over $110 trillion — with the US market commanding roughly half. Markets exist in both exchange and OTC formats, and understanding where price is formed helps you understand how to read it.
