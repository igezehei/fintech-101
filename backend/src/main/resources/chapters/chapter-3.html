A chart is a visual record of price over time. It captures the aggregate result of every decision made by every buyer and seller in that market at that moment. Technical analysis is the interpretation of charts to make probabilistic judgments about future price behavior — it works because human psychology is consistent.

Learning Objectives

  • Read and interpret different chart types
  • Identify and interpret individual and multi-candle patterns
  • Draw trendlines and channels with precision
  • Recognize the major continuation and reversal chart patterns
  • Apply Fibonacci retracements and extensions to real charts
  • Use technical indicators (EMA, RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands) in context

3.1 Chart Types

  • Line Chart: Connects closing prices. Best for long-term trend identification. Limitation: loses all intraday range information.
  • Bar Chart (OHLC): Shows Open, High, Low, Close for each period. Full price range visible.
  • Candlestick Chart: Same OHLC data in a visually intuitive format. The body spans the Open-Close range; wicks show the High and Low extremes. Bullish candles are green; bearish are red.
  • Heikin-Ashi: Smoothed candlestick chart — good for trend visibility but cannot be used for precise entry/exit timing.

3.2 Candlestick Anatomy

  • Long body, short wicks: Strong directional conviction — buyers or sellers dominated.
  • Short body, long wicks on both sides (Doji): Indecision — buyers and sellers fought to a standstill. Signals potential reversal.
  • Long upper wick, small body: Sellers rejected the push higher. Bearish implication.
  • Long lower wick, small body (Hammer): Buyers absorbed selling pressure from the lows. Bullish implication.

Key Candlestick Patterns

Single candle: Hammer (bullish reversal), Shooting Star (bearish reversal), Doji (indecision), Marubozu (full directional dominance).

Two candles: Bullish/Bearish Engulfing (one candle's body engulfs the prior), Tweezer Tops/Bottoms (double rejection at same level), Inside Bar (volatility compression before breakout).

Three candles: Morning Star (bullish reversal — bearish, doji, bullish), Evening Star (bearish reversal), Three White Soldiers (powerful bullish momentum), Three Black Crows (powerful bearish momentum).

Important rule: No candle pattern works in isolation. Context is everything. A bullish engulfing candle at a key support level after a downtrend is meaningful; the same pattern in the middle of a range is not.

3.3 Trendlines and Channels

  • Uptrend line: Drawn under higher lows. Connects at least two significant swing lows.
  • Downtrend line: Drawn above lower highs. Connects swing highs.
  • Valid trendline rules: Must connect at least two points; three confirm it. Should not cut through candle bodies. A sharper angle (>60°) is more vulnerable to breaking.
  • Channels: A trendline plus a parallel line on the other side. Ascending (rising), descending (falling), or horizontal (range-bound).

3.4 Chart Patterns

Reversal Patterns

  • Head and Shoulders: Left shoulder, higher head, right shoulder (equal to left), neckline. Break below neckline confirms bearish reversal. Target = height of head subtracted from neckline.
  • Double Top / Double Bottom: Two touches of resistance (bearish) or support (bullish). The "M" and "W" shapes.
  • Rounding Bottom (Cup): Slow curved base forming over months. Breakout above the rim with volume signals a powerful uptrend.

Continuation Patterns

  • Flags and Pennants: After a sharp move (flagpole), price consolidates in a tight channel (flag) or converging triangle (pennant). Breakout resumes the prior trend.
  • Ascending Triangle: Flat resistance + rising support. Coiling pressure, breakout often upward.
  • Descending Triangle: Flat support + declining resistance. Breakout often downward.
  • Rising Wedge: Both lines slope up but converge. Despite rising, typically bearish.
  • Falling Wedge: Both lines slope down but converge. Despite falling, often bullish reversal.

3.5 Fibonacci Retracement and Extension

Fibonacci ratios appear repeatedly in financial markets. The key levels:

  • Retracement levels: 23.6% (shallow, strong trend), 38.2% (common pullback), 50% (psychological midpoint), 61.8% (the golden ratio — most important), 78.6% (deep, trend weakening).
  • Extension levels: 127.2%, 161.8% (most common target), 200%, 261.8% (very extended moves).

How to apply: Identify a swing high and swing low. Apply the Fibonacci tool from low to high (bullish) or high to low (bearish). The levels act as potential support or resistance.

NVIDIA Example: NVIDIA's massive run from 2023–2024 offered multiple Fibonacci-based pullback entries. After each major leg higher, the 38.2%–61.8% retracement zone provided support and re-entry opportunities.

Confluence rule: Fibonacci works best when a level aligns with a prior high/low, a trendline, or a round number. Multiple tools converging on the same level increases the probability of price reacting there.

3.6 Technical Indicators

Key caveat: All indicators are derived from price. Price is primary; indicators are secondary. Use them to confirm structure, not replace it.

  • EMA (Exponential Moving Average): More weight to recent prices. EMA(21) for short-term momentum, EMA(50) for swing trading, EMA(200) for long-term trend. Golden Cross (EMA50 above EMA200) = bullish. Death Cross = bearish.
  • RSI (14): Oscillates 0–100. Above 70 = overbought; below 30 = oversold. Most important concept: divergence — price makes a new high but RSI does not, signaling weakening momentum.
  • MACD: EMA(12) − EMA(26). MACD crossing above the signal line = bullish. Histogram shrinking = momentum decelerating. A lagging indicator — confirms direction, doesn't predict turning points.
  • Bollinger Bands: Middle band (20-SMA) ± 2 standard deviations. Narrow bands (squeeze) = volatility compression, significant move coming. "Walking the bands" = price riding the upper or lower band in a strong trend.
  • VWAP: Volume Weighted Average Price. Institutional benchmark — price above VWAP = bullish intraday bias.

Common Mistakes

  • Using indicators as entry signals without price structure context.
  • Indicator overload — six conflicting signals is worse than one understood deeply. Two or three maximum.
  • Treating chart patterns as certainties — they increase probability, not guarantee outcomes.
  • Drawing trendlines with only two points — three points confirm a trendline.
  • Ignoring timeframe — a bullish signal on the 5-minute chart doesn't override a bearish daily structure.
